## Section 1: Project Overview

### The Big Idea

What if every student in our classroom spent part of each school day genuinely excited to do math and science?

That is not a hypothetical. It is happening in classrooms that use **Learning Adventures** — a free, gamified K–5 educational platform where students explore a top-down 2D school campus, play through curriculum-aligned adventures, earn XP and achievements, and compete in friendly challenges with classmates. It looks and feels like a game. Under the hood, it is aligned to Common Core Mathematics and Next Generation Science Standards, and every session generates detailed progress data I can use to differentiate instruction.

This grant would fund the technology infrastructure, professional development, and family engagement program needed to bring Learning Adventures to every student in my classroom — and document its impact as a model for adoption across our school and district.

---

## Section 2: The Problem This Project Addresses

### Student Disengagement in Math and Science

My students are bright, curious, and capable. They are also growing up in a world where their attention is competed for, constantly, by devices, platforms, and experiences designed by the best engineers and designers in the world to be maximally engaging.

When I ask them to open a math workbook or watch a science video, I am asking them to shift from that world into something that — by comparison — feels flat, slow, and disconnected from anything they care about. For some students, academic motivation and family support carry them through that gap. For many of my students — most from low-income households, many English Language Learners, many whose families are working two jobs and can't provide hours of homework support — that gap is too wide.

The data bears this out. Our school's fourth-grade math proficiency rate sits below the state average. Science engagement, measured by classroom observation and assignment completion, drops sharply between second and fourth grade. By the time students reach middle school, many have already decided that math and science are "not for them."

**This project is about changing that story before it gets written.**

### Why Existing Tools Fall Short

Our school has tried several edtech platforms. The pattern is consistent: initial novelty, then declining use, then abandonment. The platforms that drilled skills without context lost students quickly. The ones that were genuinely engaging — games students wanted to play — either lacked curriculum alignment or were inaccessible to families without premium subscriptions.

What we have never had is a platform that combines both: a real game that students choose to play, with content aligned to the standards I am required to teach, available for free to every family, safe enough that I can recommend it to parents without reservation.

Learning Adventures is that platform.

## Section 3: Learning Adventures — The Tool This Grant Would Deploy

### What Learning Adventures Is

Learning Adventures is a free, COPPA-compliant, game-based learning platform for K–5 students. Students log in, create a customizable character, and explore a top-down 2D pixel-art school campus — navigating between subject-specific buildings and playing educational adventures inside each one.

**The Math Building (fully operational):** Students enter and find 5 game stations. Each station launches an educational math game. Completing a game earns XP, which unlocks the next station and contributes to the student's overall character level.

**41 Games and Lessons — Available Right Now:**

*Math (16 items):* Fraction Pizza Party, Math Race Rally, Number Line Ninja, Multiplication Bingo Bonanza, Equation Balance Scale, Pizza Fraction Frenzy, Shape Sorting Arcade, Math Jeopardy Junior, Counting Carnival, Geometry Builder Challenge, Money Market Madness, Time Attack Clock, Math Memory Match, Treasure Hunt Calculator, Number Monster Feeding, Multiplication Tables Adventure

*Science (21 items):* Planet Explorer Quest, Ocean Conservation Heroes, Body System Heroes, Ecosystem Building Tycoon, Fossil Dig Adventure, States of Matter Mixer, Crystal Cave Chemistry, Animal Kingdom Match, Weather Wizard Battle, Magnet Power Puzzle, Light Laboratory Escape, Plant Growing Championship, Rock Cycle Racing, Sound Wave Surfer, Ocean Depth Diver, Simple Machines Construction, Pollution Solution Squad, Volcano Explorer Lab, Water Cycle Journey, Simple Machines Lab, Ecosystem Builder

*English:* Spelling Bee Challenge
*History:* Ancient Egypt Explorer

Every game is a single HTML file — no download, no external dependencies, works offline on our school Chromebooks. Designed for 5–15 minute sessions, 70% fun / 30% explicit learning.

### What It Looks Like in Practice

**In my classroom:**
- 3 sessions per week, 20 minutes per session during independent work or enrichment time
- I use the teacher dashboard to see which students completed which games, how much XP they earned, and where they struggled
- I use those insights to form differentiated small groups: students who breezed through the fraction games move to advanced challenges; students who struggled get additional scaffolding in the next lesson

**At home:**
- Parents create accounts through the parent portal, attach their child's profile, and monitor learning activity
- The platform sends a weekly digest email summarizing what their child worked on
- Students choose to return because they want to — the XP, the character customization, the daily challenges, and the friend leaderboard create genuine motivation to log back in

**For reluctant learners:**
- The adventure-game framing lowers the psychological barrier to engaging with math and science
- Students who shut down in front of a worksheet will often engage readily when the same concept is inside an adventure they chose
- The anonymous username system means students aren't embarrassed by their progress level — everyone starts equal in the campus world

### Safety and Privacy

Learning Adventures is COPPA-compliant by architecture:
- Children use algorithmically generated anonymous usernames (e.g., "BraveEagle42") — no real names stored for minors
- 4-digit PIN login — simple enough for second-graders, safe enough for legal compliance
- No advertising. No external links. No third-party tracking. No premium content locked behind a paywall.

As a teacher at a Title I school with families from immigrant communities and families with privacy concerns, I cannot overstate how important this is. Learning Adventures is a platform I can recommend without reservation.

## Section 4: How the Grant Funds Would Be Used

The $5,000 Scholastic Education Innovation Grant would fund four specific components of this project:

### Component 1: Classroom Technology Infrastructure ($1,500)

Our school's Chromebook fleet is aging. Several devices run slowly on modern web platforms, creating frustration that undermines student engagement. This funding would:
- Upgrade RAM on 10 classroom Chromebooks via authorized service (or replace 3–4 units at the district's cost-sharing rate)
- Purchase a wireless hotspot to provide reliable connectivity during learning sessions
- Set up a dedicated learning station corner with 4 devices designated for Learning Adventures sessions

### Component 2: Family Engagement Program ($1,500)

Students who engage with Learning Adventures at home as well as at school show stronger results. This funding would:
- Host 2 Family Learning Nights (evening events) where parents learn how to set up the parent dashboard, monitor their child's progress, and support learning at home
- Produce a take-home onboarding packet for every family (printed, full-color) with platform setup instructions in English and Spanish
- Provide a small stipend for a student "Tech Ambassador" (a high-achieving student volunteer) who helps classmates and their families get set up on the platform

### Component 3: Classroom Documentation and Sharing ($1,000)

This grant would position our classroom as a model implementation for the school and district. Funding would:
- Support a formal pre/post assessment process (teacher-designed, standards-aligned) to document student learning gains
- Create a classroom implementation journal documenting strategies, challenges, and adaptations across the year
- Produce a one-page implementation guide to share with other teachers at our school and at the district's spring professional development day
- Cover costs for presenting at our district's educator showcase

### Component 4: Content and Enrichment Expansion ($1,000)

Learning Adventures' content library is actively expanding — new games and lessons are added regularly. This funding would:
- Subscribe to the platform's premium tier to access early content releases and advanced analytics
- Curate a "classroom adventure path" — a custom sequence of games that maps to our unit progression across the year, shared with Learning Adventures for potential inclusion as a featured teacher path
- Purchase supplemental physical materials (manipulatives, books) that connect Learning Adventures content to hands-on classroom activities

### Reserve / Contingency ($0)

The budget is fully allocated. Any savings from Component 1 (if devices require less upgrade than estimated) would roll into expanded Family Learning Night attendance (translation services, childcare support for evening events).

## Section 5: Alignment with Scholastic's Mission

Scholastic has spent over 100 years putting books, stories, and knowledge into the hands of children — particularly children from communities where access to quality reading and learning materials is not guaranteed. The Scholastic Education Innovation Grant exists to extend that mission into classroom innovation: funding teachers who are finding new ways to reach students who need it most.

This project is in direct conversation with that legacy.

**Literacy connection:** Learning Adventures is not just a math and science platform. The reading and comprehension demands embedded in adventure narratives, game instructions, NPC dialogue, and progress reports develop ELA skills organically. Students who are reluctant readers often engage with in-game text more readily than traditional reading assignments — a phenomenon well-documented in game-based learning research.

**Story and imagination:** Like the best Scholastic books, Learning Adventures puts students inside a story — as the protagonist. The campus world, the character, the quests, and the economy are a narrative. Students are not completing exercises; they are going on adventures. That is Scholastic's DNA, applied to digital learning.

**Equity of access:** Scholastic's book clubs and book fairs have long been a mechanism for getting books into homes that wouldn't otherwise have them. Learning Adventures is built on the same principle: every educational game is free, every family gets the same access, no child is excluded by their family's income.
