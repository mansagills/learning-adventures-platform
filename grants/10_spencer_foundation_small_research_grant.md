## Abstract

This study investigates whether and how engagement with Learning Adventures — a fully operational, game-based K–5 educational platform featuring a navigable 2D campus world, curriculum-aligned STEM adventures, and gamified progression mechanics — affects students' STEM interest, STEM identity, and mathematics knowledge. Using a mixed-methods design combining validated quantitative instruments with in-depth qualitative inquiry, the study will produce conceptually grounded evidence about the mechanisms by which specific game design features — exploration, character agency, visible progression, and social belonging — contribute to or detract from meaningful STEM learning and identity development in elementary school students. Findings will advance theoretical understanding of game-based learning and inform the design of educational technology for underrepresented student populations.

## Section 1: The Research Question

### 1.1 What We Don't Know — and Why It Matters

The promise of game-based learning has been discussed in education research for two decades. The evidence base, however, remains surprisingly thin — particularly on the question of **mechanism**: not just *whether* educational games improve outcomes, but *which specific game design features* drive those improvements, for *which students*, and through *what psychological processes*.

This gap matters because it leaves designers, researchers, and practitioners without the conceptual tools needed to build better educational games. Without mechanism knowledge, game design for learning remains largely intuitive — iterative product development without a theoretical foundation.

The present study addresses this gap through a close examination of Learning Adventures, a platform that offers an unusually rich site for this inquiry. Unlike most educational games — which are thin games with content pasted on top — Learning Adventures is a genuine game world: navigable, social, character-driven, progression-structured, and economically complex. It operationalizes several theoretically distinct game design features in ways that can be studied separately and in combination.

### 1.2 The Central Research Question

**Does engagement with a game-world–based educational platform (Learning Adventures) increase K–5 students' STEM interest, STEM identity, and mathematics knowledge — and if so, through what mechanisms?**

**Sub-questions:**

1. Do students who engage more frequently with Learning Adventures show greater gains in mathematics knowledge over an 8-week period, compared to students who engage less frequently?

2. Does Learning Adventures engagement predict changes in students' STEM interest and identity, as measured by validated survey instruments?

3. Which specific platform features — the 2D campus world exploration, XP/leveling progression, character customization, social features (leaderboards, study groups), or specific game types — are most strongly associated with engagement and outcome gains?

4. How do students describe their psychological experience of learning within the platform — particularly the relationship between game enjoyment and felt learning?

5. Do effects and experiences differ by gender, race/ethnicity, or English learner status?

## Section 2: Theoretical Framework

### 2.1 The Identity-in-Practice Framework (Nasir & Hand, 2008)

Learning Adventures is studied through the lens of Nasir and Hand's identity-in-practice framework, which holds that identity is not a fixed property of persons but an ongoing accomplishment — constituted through participation in practices, including the practices of playing and learning.

From this perspective, the question is not simply whether Learning Adventures "teaches" math more effectively than other methods, but whether sustained engagement with the platform creates conditions under which students practice being STEM learners — and whether that practiced identity has durable effects on their engagement with STEM beyond the platform.

The platform's design features are analytically interesting from this framework precisely because they are identity-shaping devices:
- **Character creation and customization** — students literally construct a representation of themselves in a STEM world
- **Campus navigation** — students practice being present in a world organized around academic subjects
- **XP and leveling** — students practice being someone who grows in STEM capability
- **Social features** — students practice being part of a community of STEM learners

Each of these practices is a potential site of identity formation. The research question asks whether, and for whom, the practices "take."

### 2.2 Interest Development Theory (Hidi & Renninger, 2006)

Learning Adventures maps predictably onto Hidi and Renninger's four-phase model:
- **Triggered situational interest:** The visual world, character choice, and adventure framing create initial engagement
- **Maintained situational interest:** XP, daily challenges, and social features maintain engagement across sessions
- **Emerging individual interest:** Repeated positive experiences begin to shift self-concept
- **Well-developed individual interest:** Students seek out STEM content independently

The study will assess which phase participants occupy at baseline and at study end, and whether the platform successfully moves students across phase transitions — particularly for students who begin with low or negative STEM interest.

### 2.3 Self-Determination Theory (Deci & Ryan, 1985)

Three basic psychological needs — autonomy, competence, and relatedness — are theorized to be the proximal drivers of intrinsic motivation. Learning Adventures operationalizes all three:
- **Autonomy:** Free exploration of the campus, choice of game path, character customization, economic management
- **Competence:** Adaptive difficulty, immediate feedback, XP progression, badge achievements
- **Relatedness:** Study groups, friend challenges, shared leaderboards, NPC interactions

The study will measure whether engagement with the platform predicts satisfaction of these three needs, and whether need satisfaction mediates the relationship between platform engagement and STEM interest/identity outcomes.

### 2.4 The "Serious Game" Design Question

A persistent critique of educational games is that game mechanics function as "extrinsic motivators" — getting students to tolerate educational content they wouldn't engage with otherwise, without producing durable intrinsic motivation or identity change. This critique, if valid, would suggest that Learning Adventures' effects on STEM interest and identity are shallow — dependent on the game environment and non-transferable beyond it.

The study is designed to test this critique directly. By measuring STEM interest and identity — not just content knowledge — and by conducting qualitative inquiry into students' subjective experience of the platform and its relationship to their broader sense of themselves as learners, the study will generate evidence about whether Learning Adventures produces durable identity effects or merely context-specific engagement.

## Section 3: Research Design

### 3.1 Design Overview

**Type:** Mixed-methods — quantitative (survey + behavioral) + qualitative (in-depth interviews + classroom observation)
**Duration:** 18 months (8 weeks of data collection; 10 months analysis, writing, dissemination)
**Sample:** 150–200 students (grades 2–5) across 6–8 classrooms in 3–4 schools

### 3.2 Site Selection

Schools will be selected to ensure demographic diversity, including:
- At least 2 Title I schools (≥50% students from low-income households)
- At least 40% Black or Latino student enrollment across the sample
- Mix of urban and suburban settings
- Representation of both high-engagement and low-engagement ELL populations

### 3.3 Participants

- **Students:** 150–200, grades 2–5
- **Teachers:** 6–8, participating in data collection and brief interviews
- **Parents:** Invited to complete baseline and end-of-study surveys (voluntary)

### 3.4 Procedure

**Week 0 (Baseline):**
- Administer STEM Interest Survey and STEM Identity Survey to all participants
- Administer Math Knowledge pre-assessment (20-item researcher-developed measure, Common Core aligned, grade-differentiated)
- Platform accounts created; teacher professional development (3 hours)
- Student introduction to platform (guided 30-minute session)

**Weeks 1–8 (Implementation):**
- Students use Learning Adventures a minimum of 3 sessions per week, 20 minutes per session (in-class time)
- Platform behavioral data collected continuously: sessions, time-on-task, content completed, XP earned, voluntary logins, feature usage (world vs. game vs. social features)
- Classroom observations (researcher): Weeks 2, 5, 8 — 45-minute structured observations per classroom
- Student think-alouds (subsample of 20 students, selected for demographic diversity): 20-minute recorded sessions in Weeks 3 and 7

**Week 8 (Post-Assessment):**
- Administer STEM Interest Survey, STEM Identity Survey, and Math Knowledge post-assessment
- Student experience survey (12 items, age-appropriate Likert scale)
- Teacher survey (implementation fidelity + perceived student experience)

**Weeks 9–12 (Qualitative Follow-Up):**
- In-depth student interviews: 20 students selected from across engagement quartiles and demographic subgroups; 30–40 minutes each; semi-structured protocol
- Teacher interviews: all 6–8 teachers; 45 minutes each; semi-structured
- Parent survey analysis

### 3.5 Measures

**Quantitative:**

| Construct | Instrument | Source |
|---|---|---|
| STEM Interest | STEM-CIS (adapted, grades 2–5 version) | Kier et al., 2014 |
| STEM Identity | STEM Identity Scale (adapted) | Chemers et al., 2011 |
| Math Knowledge | Researcher-developed, 20 items, Common Core aligned | Original |
| Platform Engagement | Session logs: frequency, duration, completion, feature use | Learning Adventures platform |
| Need Satisfaction | Basic Psychological Needs Scale (adapted for age) | Deci & Ryan |
| Student Experience | Researcher-developed, 12 items | Original |

**Qualitative:**

| Data Source | Protocol Focus |
|---|---|
| Student think-alouds (Weeks 3, 7) | Real-time experience narration; what is the student attending to, feeling, thinking? |
| Student in-depth interviews (Week 9–12) | STEM identity narrative; relationship between platform and broader self-concept |
| Classroom observations (Weeks 2, 5, 8) | Engagement quality, peer interactions, help-seeking behavior, off-task patterns |
| Teacher interviews | Implementation fidelity, perceived student experience, observed identity changes |

### 3.6 Analysis Plan

**Quantitative:**
- Paired t-tests and effect sizes (Cohen's d) for pre/post changes in STEM interest, STEM identity, and math knowledge
- Regression analysis: Does platform engagement (frequency, time-on-task) predict outcome gains, controlling for grade, gender, race/ethnicity, ELL status, and baseline scores?
- Mediation analysis: Does need satisfaction mediate the relationship between platform engagement and STEM interest/identity outcomes?
- Feature-specific analysis: Correlation between usage of specific platform features (world exploration, social features, specific game types) and outcome gains

**Qualitative:**
- Thematic analysis (Braun & Clarke, 2006) of student interviews, think-alouds, and teacher interviews
- Specifically focused on: (1) identity narratives — how do students describe themselves as learners before and after? (2) mechanism narratives — what do students say drove their engagement? (3) disconfirming cases — what do low-engagement students say?
- Cross-method integration: Quantitative patterns will generate hypotheses tested in qualitative analysis; qualitative themes will generate post-hoc quantitative exploration

### 3.7 Rigor and Limitations

**Rigor measures:**
- Validated instruments with documented psychometric properties
- Member checking on qualitative interpretations (teachers review and respond to preliminary findings)
- Negative case analysis in qualitative coding (actively seeking disconfirming evidence)
- Transparent reporting of effect sizes and confidence intervals (not just p-values)
- Pre-registration of primary quantitative hypotheses (AsPredicted.org)

**Limitations:**
- Single-arm design (no randomized control group) limits causal inference — findings should be interpreted as correlational evidence of mechanism, not causal proof of efficacy
- 8-week study period limits ability to observe durable identity change — follow-up study would be needed
- Sample limited to 3–4 schools; generalizability constrained accordingly
- These limitations are appropriate for a Small Research Grant scope and are explicitly acknowledged in the proposal

## Section 4: Significance and Contribution

### 4.1 Contribution to Theory

This study contributes to learning sciences theory in three ways:

1. **Mechanism evidence for game-based learning:** By linking specific platform features to specific psychological processes (need satisfaction, identity practices, interest development phase transitions), the study goes beyond "does it work?" to "what works, for whom, and why?" — the kind of knowledge that advances both theory and design practice.

2. **Identity-in-practice in digital learning environments:** The study applies the identity-in-practice framework to a novel context — a game world — extending the framework's theoretical reach and generating new hypotheses about the conditions under which digital learning environments support or undermine identity formation.

3. **Testing the "extrinsic motivation" critique:** By measuring STEM interest and identity (not just knowledge), and by conducting qualitative inquiry into the relationship between game enjoyment and felt learning, the study directly tests the most persistent theoretical objection to game-based learning — that game mechanics produce engagement without identity change.

### 4.2 Contribution to Practice

Research findings will be translated into:
- A publicly available, plain-language report for educators and parents
- A feature-specific design guide for educational game developers: which design choices most reliably support STEM interest and identity development?
- A submission to the Journal of the Learning Sciences or the American Educational Research Journal (open-access where possible)
- A presentation at AERA Annual Meeting or ISTE

### 4.3 Contribution to the Field

The educational technology market desperately needs more rigorous research on game-based learning — not just product efficacy studies, but mechanism studies that help the field understand *why* certain design approaches work. This study provides that.

It is worth noting that Learning Adventures is unusual as a research site: it is a complete, fully operational platform rather than a purpose-built laboratory tool. Findings about how real students engage with a real platform, in real classroom conditions, have higher ecological validity than findings from controlled lab studies — and are more directly actionable for practitioners.
