## Section 1: Overview and Significance

### 1.1 The Challenge

The United States faces a sustained and worsening shortage of STEM-prepared workers. The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects that STEM occupations will grow at nearly twice the rate of non-STEM fields through 2030, yet the pipeline of students who identify with STEM, who see themselves as future scientists, technologists, engineers, or mathematicians, is narrowing — particularly among students from underrepresented groups.

The roots of this problem reach deep into elementary school. By grade 5, children have already formed durable beliefs about whether math is "for them," whether science is something they can do, and whether technology-related careers are accessible futures. These beliefs are not neutral — they are shaped by the quality and character of a child's early STEM experiences. When those experiences are rote, passive, and disconnected from the child's identity and interests, the pipeline loses students before it begins.

ITEST was created to address exactly this problem: to develop, test, and scale innovative technology experiences that expand K–12 students' interest in and preparation for STEM and information and communications technology (ICT) careers. **Learning Adventures is an ITEST-aligned innovation of exceptional depth and operational readiness.**

### 1.2 The Opportunity

Learning Adventures is a fully operational, game-based STEM learning platform for K–5 students. It combines:

- A **top-down 2D pixel-art campus world** (built on Phaser.js + Next.js 14) that students explore with a customizable avatar, in the visual tradition of Stardew Valley and classic Zelda
- **41 curriculum-aligned STEM games and interactive lessons** across Mathematics, Science, English, History, and Interdisciplinary subjects — all available today, all verified functional
- An **XP, badge, and achievement system** tied directly to STEM learning milestones
- **Social features** (leaderboards, study groups, friend challenges) that leverage peer motivation
- **Parent and teacher dashboards** that surface learning data to the adults who support each child
- A **Gemini AI–powered content creation studio** that enables rapid expansion of the game library in response to identified learning gaps
- **COPPA-compliant child authentication** with anonymous usernames and PIN-based login — designed safe for children from day one

The platform is not a concept. It is built, operational, and ready for rigorous research study. This project proposes to deploy Learning Adventures across a diverse sample of K–5 classrooms and after-school programs, conduct a rigorous mixed-methods study of its impact on student STEM interest, identity, and skill development, and use findings to refine the platform and publish a scalable implementation model.

## Section 2: Project Goals and Research Questions

### 2.1 Project Goals

**Goal 1: Expand STEM Interest and Identity Among K–5 Students**
Demonstrate that game-based learning experiences in Learning Adventures increase students' expressed interest in STEM subjects and their identification as capable STEM learners, with particular emphasis on girls, students of color, English language learners, and students from low-income households.

**Goal 2: Build Early STEM Skills and Conceptual Understanding**
Demonstrate measurable improvements in math and science knowledge and skills among students who regularly engage with Learning Adventures, as measured by validated pre/post assessments aligned to Common Core Mathematics Standards and Next Generation Science Standards (NGSS).

**Goal 3: Increase Educator Confidence and Capacity**
Support participating teachers in integrating game-based learning into their instructional practice, building their capacity to leverage technology for differentiated, engaging STEM instruction.

**Goal 4: Develop a Scalable, Evidence-Based Implementation Model**
Produce a replicable implementation framework — including educator training protocols, platform integration guides, and outcome measurement tools — that enables other schools and districts to adopt Learning Adventures effectively.

### 2.2 Research Questions

**Primary Research Questions:**

1. To what extent does engagement with the Learning Adventures platform increase K–5 students' interest in and positive identity around STEM subjects, compared to students in comparison classrooms?

2. What are the effects of Learning Adventures on students' mathematical reasoning and scientific thinking skills, as measured by validated assessments?

3. How do student demographic characteristics (gender, race/ethnicity, socioeconomic status, English language learner status) moderate the platform's effects on STEM interest and skill development?

**Secondary Research Questions:**

4. What game design features and learning mechanics drive the highest levels of student engagement, as measured by behavioral data (sessions, time-on-task, completion rates, voluntary return) and qualitative student interviews?

5. What implementation factors — teacher preparation, platform integration approach, session frequency, family engagement — are associated with stronger student outcomes?

6. How does the platform's COPPA-compliant design, anonymous social features, and parent visibility model affect family engagement and trust?

## Section 3: Theoretical Framework

### 3.1 Interest Development Theory

Learning Adventures is designed around Hidi and Renninger's (2006) four-phase model of interest development:
- **Triggered situational interest** — The visual world, character customization, and adventure framing immediately capture attention
- **Maintained situational interest** — XP progression, daily challenges, and social features sustain engagement session-to-session
- **Emerging individual interest** — Repeated positive experiences with STEM content begin to shift self-concept
- **Well-developed individual interest** — Students begin to seek out STEM content independently, outside the platform

The platform's design is explicitly intended to move students through these phases. The research study will measure which design elements are most effective at each phase transition.

### 3.2 Self-Determination Theory

Learning Adventures operationalizes the three core psychological needs identified by Deci and Ryan (1985):
- **Autonomy** — Students choose which building to enter, which game to play, how to customize their character, and how to manage their in-game economy
- **Competence** — Adaptive difficulty, immediate feedback, and visible XP progression provide continuous, calibrated challenge
- **Relatedness** — Study groups, friend challenges, leaderboards, and shared milestones embed STEM learning in a social context

### 3.3 Culturally Responsive Game Design

Learning Adventures was designed to be culturally affirming through:
- Diverse avatar options (human, fantasy, and animal sprites) avoiding default whiteness
- Subject matter that affirms the scientific contributions of diverse cultures (e.g., Ancient Egypt Explorer, Ocean Conservation Heroes)
- A narrative frame — school as an adventure world — that recontextualizes formal learning as exciting rather than burdensome
- COPPA compliance that protects children's privacy and builds parental trust across communities with justified concerns about tech surveillance

### 3.4 Computational Thinking and ICT Career Pathways

Beyond subject-matter learning, Learning Adventures develops foundational computational thinking skills through:
- Game mechanics that require sequential reasoning (step-by-step problem solving)
- Patterns and abstraction (identifying mathematical patterns, generalizing scientific rules)
- Algorithmic thinking (optimizing strategies in economy and job mini-games)
- Data interpretation (reading the progress dashboard, understanding XP and leveling logic)

These are the cognitive skills identified by the Computer Science Teachers Association (CSTA) and NSF as foundational for future engagement with computer science and ICT careers.

## Section 4: Project Design and Methods

### 4.1 Participant Recruitment

**Year 1 (Pilot):**
- 8–10 elementary schools (Title I priority)
- 3–5 grade levels (K–5)
- Approximately 40 teachers, 800 students
- Target: ≥50% students from underrepresented groups in STEM

**Years 2–3 (Expansion):**
- 25–30 schools across 3–5 states
- Approximately 100 teachers, 2,500 students
- Geographic diversity: urban, suburban, and rural settings

**Year 4 (Sustainability & Dissemination):**
- 50+ schools
- Transition to independent implementation with research team in advisory role

### 4.2 Study Design

**Randomized Controlled Trial (RCT) — Cluster Randomized:**
Schools will be randomly assigned to treatment (Learning Adventures) or waitlist control conditions. Control schools will receive access to the platform after Year 1 data collection, ensuring ethical access for all participants.

**Mixed-Methods Data Collection:**

| Data Source | Instrument | Frequency |
|---|---|---|
| STEM Interest & Identity | Validated survey (STEM-CIS or MSIS adapted) | Pre, mid, post |
| Math Knowledge | NWEA MAP or researcher-developed items aligned to Common Core | Pre and post |
| Science Knowledge | Researcher-developed items aligned to NGSS | Pre and post |
| Platform Behavioral Data | Session logs, completion rates, time-on-task, voluntary engagement | Continuous |
| Teacher Practice | Observation protocol + teacher survey | 3x/year |
| Student Experience | Focus groups + individual interviews | 2x/year |
| Parent Engagement | Survey + dashboard usage analytics | Pre and post |
| Implementation Fidelity | Structured observation + log review | Monthly |

### 4.3 Platform Integration Models

The study will test three integration models to identify the most effective approach:

**Model A: Supplemental Enrichment (15–20 min/day, 3 days/week)**
Students use Learning Adventures as a supplemental activity during independent work time or enrichment periods. Teacher monitors progress via dashboard but does not direct in-platform activities.

**Model B: Blended Instruction (30 min/day, 5 days/week)**
Teacher integrates Learning Adventures as a core component of math/science instruction. Pre-game discussion → adventure session → post-game reflection. Teacher uses dashboard data to inform instructional groupings.

**Model C: After-School Program**
Learning Adventures deployed as an after-school STEM club, 3 days/week, facilitated by a trained after-school coordinator. Emphasis on social features, daily challenges, and family engagement.

### 4.4 Educator Professional Development

All participating teachers will receive:
- **Initial training (8 hours):** Platform navigation, dashboard use, integration strategies, discussion facilitation
- **Ongoing coaching (monthly):** Virtual coaching sessions with research team; co-reflection on student data
- **Peer learning community:** Cross-school community of practice, sharing implementation strategies
- **Content development workshops (Year 2+):** Teachers contribute ideas for new adventures through Learning Adventures' AI content studio — building teacher agency and investment

### 4.5 Family Engagement

- **Parent onboarding:** Each family receives a guided setup session for the parent dashboard (in-person at school or virtual)
- **Weekly family digests:** Automated summaries of child's learning activity sent to parent email or text
- **Family challenge events:** Monthly events where children can complete special adventures with family members
- **Multilingual support:** Dashboard and communications translated to Spanish (Year 1), French and Mandarin (Year 2)

## Section 5: Platform Technical Specifications

### 5.1 Architecture

| Component | Technology |
|---|---|
| Frontend Framework | Next.js 14, React, TypeScript |
| Game Engine | Phaser.js 3 (integrated into Next.js via React-Phaser bridge) |
| Authentication | NextAuth.js v4 (JWT strategy, OAuth + credentials) |
| Database | PostgreSQL 14 + Prisma ORM |
| AI Content Studio | Google Gemini API, multi-agent workflow architecture |
| Styling | Tailwind CSS |
| Deployment | Vercel (frontend) + PostgreSQL hosting |
| Research Data Export | REST API endpoints for anonymized behavioral data export |

### 5.2 Data Privacy & Security

- **COPPA-compliant by design:** Children's accounts attached to parent accounts; no PII stored for minors; anonymous username system (e.g., "BraveEagle42")
- **FERPA-aware:** Student educational records handled per FERPA requirements; data sharing agreements with participating schools
- **Data minimization:** Only data necessary for platform function and research is collected
- **Encryption:** All data encrypted at rest and in transit
- **Research data:** De-identified behavioral logs exported through secure API for research team; no individual child identification possible in research dataset

### 5.3 Accessibility

- WCAG 2.1 AA compliance targeted across all platform components
- Keyboard navigation for all interactive elements
- High-contrast mode available
- Screen reader optimization (ongoing)
- Mobile-responsive design for tablet and phone use

## Section 7: Broader Impacts

### Advancing STEM Education Research
This project will produce the first rigorous RCT of a fully game-based, open-world STEM learning platform for K–5 students, generating novel evidence about the mechanisms by which game design features drive STEM interest and skill development.

### Broadening Participation in STEM
By targeting Title I schools and measuring heterogeneity of treatment effects by demographic subgroup, the project directly advances NSF's goal of broadening participation in STEM among underrepresented groups.

### Building Educator Capacity
The professional development program and teacher community of practice will equip 100+ educators with the skills and confidence to integrate technology meaningfully into STEM instruction — a capacity that persists well beyond the project period.

### Scalable Open Infrastructure
Learning Adventures' AI-powered content studio enables the platform to scale content coverage without proportional cost increases. The implementation framework produced in Year 4 will allow any school or district to adopt the model with fidelity.

### Public Engagement
The platform's family-facing components — parent dashboards, weekly digests, family challenge events — extend STEM engagement beyond school hours, building the home-school connection that research consistently identifies as a key driver of student achievement.
