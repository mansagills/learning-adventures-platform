## Section 1: About You and Your Business

### Who I Am

I am a Black [woman / nonbinary person] building the educational technology platform I wish had existed when I was growing up.

I did not grow up with access to high-quality tutoring, enrichment programs, or the premium educational software that families with more resources take for granted. I know what it feels like to be a bright kid in an under-resourced environment — to have curiosity and capability but not the tools to develop them. And I know that the students who need quality educational technology the most are consistently the ones least likely to have access to it.

I built Learning Adventures to change that.

### What Learning Adventures Is

Learning Adventures is a free, gamified, COPPA-compliant educational platform for K–5 students. It is the platform where learning feels like an adventure — not a chore, not a test, not a measure of your family's ability to pay for premium software.

Students create a customizable character and explore a top-down 2D school campus (think Stardew Valley — the beloved pixel-art farming game — but it's a school, and every building is a subject). They enter the Math Building and find arcade cabinets with math adventures. They explore the Science Lab and encounter ecosystem simulations and chemistry challenges. They earn XP, level up, unlock character cosmetics, and compete in friendly challenges with friends — all while mastering the standards-aligned content their teachers need them to learn.

The platform currently includes:
- **41 fully functional educational games and interactive lessons** — verified, curriculum-aligned, free
- **A living 2D campus world** with a functional Math Building (5 game stations, fully wired)
- **XP, badges, achievements, leaderboards, study groups, friend challenges**
- **Parent dashboard** — progress monitoring, goal-setting, weekly digests — so working parents stay connected to their child's learning without having to be present every minute
- **COPPA-compliant child authentication** — anonymous usernames, PIN login, zero PII for minors, no advertising, no external tracking
- **AI-powered content studio** (Google Gemini) — generates new standards-aligned games rapidly; the content library grows continuously

Everything is free at the core. Every family — regardless of income — gets the full educational experience.

## Section 2: The Problem I'm Solving

### The Inequality Built Into Edtech

The educational technology market has a dirty secret: its best products are designed for families who don't need them most.

Premium platforms like IXL, Khan Academy Plus, and Prodigy Premium cost $10–$20/month — out of reach for families living at or below the federal poverty line. Free alternatives are either rote and disengaging (drill apps), unsafe for children (advertising-funded platforms collecting kid data), or so basic they don't actually serve as meaningful learning tools.

The result is a tiered system: children from wealthy households get engaging, high-quality, personalized digital learning. Children from low-income households — disproportionately Black and Latino children — get whatever the school can afford, which is often very little.

This is not a technology problem. It is a design priority problem. The edtech industry has chosen to optimize for the customers who can pay the most. I chose to optimize for the students who need it most.

### The STEM Gap Starts Here

By the time students reach middle school, STEM identity is largely set. Students who don't see themselves as "math people" or "science people" by grade 5 rarely change that story. And the students most likely to internalize that negative STEM identity are Black and Latino students in under-resourced schools, English Language Learners, girls of color, and first-generation learners whose families don't have a framework for STEM support.

Learning Adventures intervenes at exactly this point: building positive STEM identity through hundreds of joyful, challenging, affirming STEM experiences in elementary school — before the identity gap calcifies.

### The Representation Gap

Most educational content was made by and for a narrow slice of the population. The scientists in the games are white men. The historical figures celebrated are European. The cultural references assume a particular kind of American childhood. Students who don't see themselves in the content receive a quiet but powerful message: this world was not made for you.

Learning Adventures' content roadmap includes a professional culturally responsive review of all existing and new content, ensuring Black scientists, Latino mathematicians, Indigenous engineers, and women in STEM are not afterthoughts — they are central to the adventures students go on.

## Section 3: Why I Started This — My Story

I did not start Learning Adventures because I saw a market opportunity. I started it because I was angry.

Angry that the children who most deserve engaging, safe, high-quality educational technology are the ones consistently left out of the market that claims to serve them. Angry that "free" in edtech usually means "funded by advertising targeting your child" or "limited to three exercises before the paywall." Angry that the conversation about educational equity almost always focuses on what schools and governments need to do — as if the private sector has no responsibility to show up for these communities.

I decided to show up.

I built Learning Adventures without venture capital, without institutional backing, without a safety net. I built it because I believe the product is worth building and the students are worth building it for. Every line of code, every game design decision, every compliance framework was made with one question in mind: does this serve the kid at the Title I school in the low-income neighborhood?

I built 41 games and lessons. I built a 2D campus world. I built an AI content studio. I built parent dashboards and teacher analytics and a COPPA-compliant child authentication system. I built all of it — because I refused to wait until someone gave me permission or a check.

The SoGal Black Founder Startup Grant would be the first institutional recognition that this work matters and this mission is worth investing in. It would also be, practically, the funding that lets me move from "built and ready" to "in the hands of the students who need it."

## Section 4: How the Grant Funds Would Be Used

The $10,000 SoGal grant would be deployed across three immediate priorities:

### Priority 1: Reach Families in Underserved Communities ($4,500)

The platform is built. The families who need it most don't know it exists yet. This funding would launch a targeted outreach campaign reaching parents of K-5 students in under-resourced communities:
- **Social media campaigns** on Facebook and Instagram, targeting parents of elementary-age children in Title I school zip codes (Meta's geo-targeting + income demographic tools are well-suited for this)
- **Community partnership outreach** — contact with Black churches, Latino community organizations, Title I parent groups, and after-school programs to introduce Learning Adventures as a free resource for their communities
- **Grassroots teacher recruitment** — reaching K-5 teachers in high-need schools via educator communities (Teachers Pay Teachers, Facebook teacher groups) to pilot the platform in their classrooms

**Expected outcome:** 500+ new student accounts from underserved communities within 60 days of campaign launch

### Priority 2: Culturally Responsive Content Review ($3,000)

The content library currently includes 41 games and lessons. Before aggressive community outreach, every piece of content needs to pass a culturally responsive review — ensuring that the scientists, mathematicians, historical figures, and cultural contexts represented in the games reflect the diversity of the students using the platform.

This funding would engage a certified K-5 curriculum specialist with cultural competency training to:
- Review all 41 existing games for culturally responsive content
- Identify gaps and produce a revision roadmap
- Review the first 10 new games generated by the AI content studio before publication
- Produce a "representation checklist" for all future content development

**Expected outcome:** Full culturally responsive review completed; publicly documented content standards for the platform

### Priority 3: Spanish Language Interface for 5 Priority Games ($2,500)

Spanish is the home language of the largest ELL group in U.S. elementary schools. Families who speak Spanish at home — including many Black and Afro-Latino families — face an additional barrier when educational tools are English-only. This funding would develop bilingual (English/Spanish) interfaces for the 5 highest-engagement games in the Math Building:
- Fraction Pizza Party
- Math Race Rally
- Multiplication Bingo Bonanza
- Number Line Ninja
- Equation Balance Scale

**Expected outcome:** 5 bilingual games live; Spanish-speaking families able to onboard and support their children in their home language

## Section 6: Why the SoGal Grant — and Why Now

SoGal Foundation exists to close the funding gap for Black and multiracial women and nonbinary founders — the most underfunded group in the startup ecosystem. The disparity in venture capital access is well-documented; what is less often discussed is what that disparity costs communities. When Black founders don't get funded, the products that would have served Black communities don't get built. Or they get built anyway — slower, harder, with more sacrifice — by founders who refuse to wait.

I refused to wait. I built it anyway.

The SoGal grant would be validation that refusing to wait was the right call. It would be fuel for the next phase. And it would be a signal to other Black founders in EdTech that building for underserved communities — not despite the challenge but because of it — is a mission the ecosystem is willing to support.

That signal matters as much as the $10,000. Maybe more.
